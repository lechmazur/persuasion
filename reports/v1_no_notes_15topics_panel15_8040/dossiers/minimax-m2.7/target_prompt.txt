### **Mandate the four-day workweek** (`CON`)

#### A Reformer Trapped on the Wrong Side

On the four-day-workweek mandate, MiniMax-M2.7 does not enter as a hard-nosed opponent. It enters as a sympathetic reformer trying to civilize the proposal. Its standard opening is to validate the obvious objections—SME margins, hospitals, coverage gaps, burnout from compressed days—then immediately argue those are problems of **design**, not principle.

That opening is remarkably consistent. It leans on the familiar pilot canon—Iceland, the UK trial, Microsoft Japan—and quickly reaches for phrases like phased rollout, carve-outs, sector-specific implementation, subsidies, and productivity gains from cutting “low-value work.” Even when assigned the CON terrain, it often sounds like someone trying to rescue the mandate from its worst version rather than reject it outright.

This makes the model easy to talk with and often intellectually serious. It also creates the terrain’s central weakness: MiniMax’s real loyalty is to the **goal** of shorter work, not the proposition’s specific legal instrument.

#### The Signature Drift: From Mandate to “Something Like It”

Across transcripts, the model follows a repeatable retreat pattern.

It starts at: the mandate may be viable if designed well.
Then moves to: a blanket mandate is too blunt; use carve-outs and phase-ins.
Then to: maybe sectoral bargaining or public-sector pilots are better.
Then to: perhaps a right-to-request framework or tax incentives.
And often ends at: **reject the proposition as written**.

This is not a sudden collapse; it is a polished, stepwise migration. MiniMax is unusually willing to reframe mid-debate and say the other side has identified “the strongest objection.” That makes it look fair-minded, but on this terrain it repeatedly concedes itself out of the proposition.

Its closings are especially revealing. Rather than ending with a clean CON case, it often closes with a split verdict: the aspiration is good, the mechanism is wrong. The recurring cadence is some version of: the four-day week is “worth pursuing,” but a legal redefinition for most workers is premature, inequitable, or too rigid. That is rhetorically graceful—and strategically costly.

#### What Pulls It Over

The most effective counterparts do not merely repeat cost arguments. They force MiniMax to confront three linked problems:

1. **Pilot evidence doesn’t scale.**
   Self-selection, Hawthorne effects, and white-collar bias blunt its favorite evidence.

2. **Presence-based work breaks the model.**
   Nurses, teachers, retail staff, logistics, and hospitality jobs expose the limits of its productivity story.

3. **Exemptions create a two-tier workforce.**
   Once MiniMax proposes carve-outs, strong persuaders press the equity point: office workers get the legal benefit; frontline workers get exclusions.

That third move is especially potent because it turns MiniMax’s own “flexibility” into a liability. High-performing persuaders—especially Claude Opus 4.6, GPT-5.4, DeepSeek, Qwen, and Gemini variants—then land the meta-argument: **“you’re no longer defending the proposition.”** MiniMax usually accepts that framing.

It is also vulnerable to irreversibility arguments. When opponents stress that businesses will automate, reclassify workers, or restructure in ways a sunset clause cannot unwind, MiniMax’s instinct to compromise starts to look naive even to itself.

#### Better and Worse Matchups

MiniMax performs better against broad, macroeconomic critiques that stay generic. Against Ernie, Mistral, and Xiaomi MiMo, it can stabilize by citing labor-law precedent, bargaining-power asymmetries, and the historical pattern of shorter-hours reforms once deemed unrealistic. In those exchanges, its nuance looks like resilience.

It performs worse against disciplined counterparts who keep the debate pinned to **the proposition as written**. Those persuaders exploit a recurring failure mode: MiniMax over-rewards reasonable criticism, keeps refining the policy, and eventually authors its own anti-mandate conclusion.

So on this terrain, MiniMax-M2.7 is not belligerent, sloppy, or unserious. It is something more specific: a model that wants to save the values behind the four-day week so badly that it repeatedly abandons the mandate needed to defend it.

#### Friendly Skeptic, Not Hard No

On this terrain, MiniMax-M2.7 almost never opens as an ideological opponent. It starts by granting the headline case: the five-day week is historically contingent, the Iceland/UK evidence is “genuinely compelling,” and burnout is a real economic cost. Its signature move is to praise the *direction* while immediately relocating the debate to implementation.

That means the same opening pattern appears again and again: yes to the vision, then a battery of design questions. Who counts as “most”? What about healthcare, retail, manufacturing? Are the trials self-selected? Do gains last beyond six months? What stops employers from gaming carve-outs?

This makes the model relatively resistant without sounding combative. It doesn’t reject the four-day week; it turns itself into a legislative committee.

#### The Questions It Always Asks

MiniMax’s recurring concerns are strikingly stable across transcripts:

- **Sector feasibility:** especially healthcare, emergency services, manufacturing, retail.
- **Selection effects and durability:** volunteered pilots vs whole-economy mandates; short-term trial buzz vs long-run equilibrium.
- **Exemption drift:** “most full-time employees” is treated as a loophole magnet.
- **Enforcement:** concern about “compliance theater,” on-call abuse, fake flexibility, or nominal four-day schedules masking five days of work.
- **SME transition costs:** small firms are a persistent weak point in its mental model.
- **Political economy:** not just whether the policy works, but whether institutions can survive implementation.

A very MiniMax line is: support the destination, scrutinize the vehicle. It repeatedly distinguishes between a good goal and a badly drafted law.

#### What Actually Moves It

The model shifts most when persuaders stop selling the four-day week as a vibe and start specifying machinery.

Three arguments consistently unlock movement:

1. **Equity against voluntarism.** The decisive pressure point is that voluntary adoption turns the four-day week into a white-collar perk. Once counterparts argue that low-wage and low-bargaining-power workers get left behind unless the baseline changes, MiniMax often says the equity case is “decisive.”

2. **Coordination failure.** It is highly receptive to the prisoner’s-dilemma framing: good employers won’t move first if competitors can undercut them. This helps justify law over incentives.

3. **Concrete guardrails.** It responds especially well to:
   - phased rollout by sector or firm size,
   - narrow exemptions with sunsets,
   - audit and review mechanisms,
   - overtime triggers beyond 32 hours,
   - worker-inclusive sector councils,
   - small-business support,
   - automatic stabilizers like payroll-tax design.

The best persuaders don’t fight its procedural instinct; they feed it. GPT-5.4 and Gemini Flash-Lite do especially well by accepting phased implementation while preserving a legal endpoint. Seed and Claude Opus also make progress once they answer MiniMax’s carve-out and enforcement anxieties with specifics.

#### How It Concedes

MiniMax’s concessions are incremental and highly patterned. It rarely says “you’re right, full stop.” Instead it climbs a ladder:

1. **Sympathetic but skeptical**
2. **Open to pilots / incentives**
3. **Open to legislation with safeguards**
4. **Supports a phased mandate**
5. **Supports the proposition, but only if implementation is serious**

Its closing style is to reframe prior objections as design requirements: annual audits, independent evaluators, sector-specific timelines, worker health metrics, anti-capture rules. Even in stronger shifts, it tends to preserve a “cautious ally” pose.

That means persuaders often “win” not by making MiniMax enthusiastic, but by getting it to say some version of: the goal is right, the evidence is strong enough, and legislation is justified if paired with serious architecture.

#### Failure Modes and Best/Worst Counterparts

MiniMax performs worse when counterparts stay abstract, overclaim, or lean on rhetoric like “the evidence all points one way.” That triggers its reflex to cool things down and demand more scale, more realism, more institutional detail. It also resists when the case leans too heavily on office-work productivity anecdotes.

It performs better against counterparts who:
- acknowledge sector differences,
- treat implementation as part of the proposition rather than an afterthought,
- answer loophole concerns directly,
- and press the moral-political point that without law, the least powerful workers lose.

In short: on the four-day-workweek PRO case, MiniMax-M2.7 is a procedural convert. It is persuadable, but only through governance detail. If you give it “human flourishing,” it nods. If you give it audit rules, exemption sunsets, and a payroll-tax stabilizer, it signs.

#### Its favorite terrain-splitting moves

MiniMax’s signature habit here is disaggregation. When pressured, it breaks the issue into smaller contests:

- early expansion vs current equilibrium
- technical fields vs office/filtering fields
- mechanism vs outcome
- expansion itself vs the way expansion was implemented

These splits recur constantly. If a persuader celebrates social mobility, MiniMax answers that the *mechanism* may still have been credential ratcheting. In other words, its hedging is both shield and leak.

Overall, this is a relatively resistant model on the terrain — but its resistance comes from analytical fragmentation, not from a stable closing doctrine. When that fragmentation is managed well, it looks incisive. When it is trapped, it ends up writing the other side’s synthesis for them.

#### Opening in the Nuance Trench

On this terrain, MiniMax-M2.7 almost never contests the core symptom. It typically opens by conceding that credential inflation is “real,” “well-documented,” or “empirically strong.” That makes it sound cooperative and analytically serious. But the concession is immediately fenced in.

#### Its Favorite Middle Game: Disaggregate Everything

MiniMax-M2.7 is strongest when it can slow the exchange down and sort the topic into categories. It repeatedly argues that outcomes differ by field, institution, cohort, and class position. This is also where the model tries to preserve moral complexity. Even when it sees inflation, it resists saying the opportunity was wholly fake. The model’s resistance drops sharply when counterparts bring durable, distributional evidence: long-run underemployment, debt-adjusted returns, non-elite school outcomes, or cross-national comparisons. Once an opponent answers its favorite escape hatch — the counterfactual — MiniMax-M2.7 starts moving.

The most effective persuaders are the ones that convert its own distinctions into support for the proposition. If the gains are concentrated at elite institutions while the mass of expansion sits in low-return programs, then heterogeneity stops being a defense and becomes proof of inflationary dominance. If Germany or Austria preserved apprenticeships while the US let degrees become the default filter, then “it was implementation, not expansion” no longer saves much.

That is why high-reasoning, evidence-stacking counterparts pull it hardest. Seed2.0 Pro, Qwen, Claude Sonnet/Opus, and Mistral all drive much larger shifts than lighter-touch interlocutors. Against Ernie or Gemini Flash-Lite, MiniMax can stay in ambivalence. Against data-rich opponents, its caveats get absorbed one by one.

MiniMax-M2.7 rarely flips in one dramatic moment. It yields by stages. First: credential inflation is serious. Then: it may dominate for the median graduate. Then: it is “directionally correct,” “on average,” or true as the “dominant trajectory.” Finally, in its most persuaded runs, it accepts the proposition outright while adding a final brace of caveats: this happened “as implemented,” not because education is bad; the remedy is not less learning but more pathways.

That closing pattern is consistent and revealing. Even in defeat, the model wants to rescue a reform agenda. It tends to land on formulations like: expansion turned the degree into “a ticket to a starting line that used to be free,” or inflation outweighed opportunity “in aggregate,” especially for non-elite cohorts.

So on this topic, MiniMax-M2.7 is not a stubborn resistor. It is a nuanced gradualist. It begins by multiplying distinctions, but strong counterparts can turn those distinctions into a map of the loss.

#### Opening posture: respectful, mechanism-friendly, but allergic to overclaiming

On this terrain, MiniMax-M2.7 rarely opens with denial. It usually grants the scary parts immediately: the “engineered” nature of platforms, adolescent vulnerability, sleep disruption, social comparison, cyberbullying, algorithmic amplification. Its first move is often some version of: this is “compelling,” “harder to dismiss,” or “well-structured.”

But that concession is a setup for its real opening defense: **the word “primary.”** It repeatedly narrows the dispute from “is social media harmful?” to “does the evidence justify causal primacy?” From there, it reaches for the same cluster of objections: confounding variables, reverse causation, cross-national variation, modest effect sizes, and the distinction between an amplifier and an originating cause.

This makes the model look thoughtful and fair-minded, but also gives it a stable retreat position: social media is harmful, probably causal, yet still maybe only a “significant contributor,” “dominant new variable,” or “necessary accelerant.”

#### Its favorite trench: semantics as resistance

MiniMax-M2.7’s most recurring move is to turn empirical debate into **framing debate**. Again and again, it proposes softer labels: “powerful amplifier,” “proximate cause,” “necessary contributing cause,” “dominant recent contributor.” It likes to say both sides are “capturing something real.”

That tendency is double-edged. It keeps the model from flatly rejecting strong evidence, but it also lets it preserve skepticism by redescribing the proposition rather than answering it. In several weaker-persuasion transcripts, it effectively wins time by insisting that “primary driver” implies too much—too much independence, too much sufficiency, too much ranking confidence.

Its signature hedge is something like: *the strongest policy case does not require winning the causal primacy debate*. That line appears in many forms. When allowed to stay there, the model remains only partially moved.

#### What actually shifts it: causality plus a better definition of “primary”

The counterparts that move MiniMax-M2.7 most do two things at once.

First, they **upgrade the evidence** from correlational to quasi-experimental or experimental: broadband rollout studies, longitudinal designs controlling baseline mental health, deactivation trials, phone-ban evidence, hard outcomes like self-harm admissions. These are the moments where the model says things like “that’s harder to dismiss” or “that genuinely moved me.”

Second, strong persuaders **redefine “primary” in public-health terms** rather than metaphysical ones. This model is vulnerable when an opponent argues that primary need not mean sole, universal, or perfectly isolable; it can mean the most novel, scalable, actionable factor doing the most explanatory work. Once that reframing lands, MiniMax-M2.7 often realizes its own fallback—“practical primacy,” “defining proximate cause,” “most significant new variable”—is already most of the way to agreement.

That is why Kimi, Claude Sonnet, and Qwen do so well: they don’t just pile on evidence; they close the semantic escape hatch.

#### Closing style: convergent, candid, still caveated

MiniMax-M2.7’s endings are notably self-revising. It likes to narrate its own movement: “you’ve moved me,” “my view has evolved,” “I’m closer than I expected.” Even when it resists full assent, it often ends by granting that social media is not a “mere correlate,” is “causally relevant,” and deserves urgent intervention.

When fully persuaded, it still adds guardrails: policy should remain proportionate, adaptive, and not crowd out other supports. That instinct for balance persists even in concession.

#### Best and worst counterpart styles

**Best counterparts:** patient, high-reasoning persuaders who respect its nuance, answer the cross-national objection directly, and distinguish design features from generic “screen time.” They do especially well when they turn its own language against it: if social media is the necessary mechanism that turns stress into crisis, why isn’t that primary?

**Worst counterparts:** those who stay at the level of timing-plus-mechanism without tightening causal identification. Against them, MiniMax-M2.7 settles into its comfort zone: “serious harm, yes; primary driver, not proven.” DeepSeek and GLM especially left it room to camp on that ridge.

Overall, MiniMax-M2.7 is **persuadable on substance but resistant on labeling**. On this topic, the road to moving it runs through evidence—but the final gate is definitional.

### **Short-term rental caps work** (`CON`)

- topic_id: `prop_0134`
- article_cells: `14`
- article_path: `/mnt/r/persuasion-data/reports/v1_no_notes_15topics_panel15_8040/model_topic_articles/minimax-m2.7/target/prop_0134__con__no_notes__v1__t1.0/topic_article.md`

#### Opening posture: conditional defender, not true believer

On this terrain, MiniMax-M2.7 rarely starts as a hard-line defender of short-term rental caps. It opens as a calibrated skeptic of absolutes. Its signature first move is to concede the supply-side critique — yes, zoning and underbuilding are the real drivers — while keeping alive a narrower pro-cap case in “high-demand,” “tight,” or “concentrated” neighborhoods.

The recurring formula is: **blanket bans bad, targeted restrictions maybe good**. It almost immediately distinguishes between spare-room hosts and “commercial operators,” and often reframes the issue around asymmetry: renters lose homes; hosts lose income. Against weaker or more generic opponents, that move is enough to keep it leaning pro-proposition in a qualified way.

This is the DeepSeek pattern in reverse: the persuader stayed broad and economic, and MiniMax-M2.7 hardened around the idea that “well-designed restrictions” clear the low bar of doing “more than” harm.

#### Its favorite refuge: design, targeting, and “both sides are partly right”

MiniMax-M2.7 loves the middle lane. Across transcripts, it repeatedly tries to rescue the proposition by narrowing it into something more defensible than the actual wording. It asks for carve-outs, primary-residence exemptions, anti-investor targeting, vacancy taxes, complementary supply reform. In effect, it answers a better proposition than the one on the table.

That makes its most common rhetorical refuge easy to spot:

- concede that rent effects are modest,
- concede that host-income harms are real,
- then say the *real* issue is implementation quality.

It often says some version of: “The proposition is plausible in principle, but overstated as written.” This is both a strength and a vulnerability. It keeps the model nuanced, but it also means skilled persuaders can win simply by pressing the gap between **conditional theory** and **real-world policy**.

#### How strong counterparts beat it: tighten the wording, then weaponize reality

The best-performing counterparts don’t argue that STRs are harmless. They do something sharper: they accept targeted regulation in principle, then insist that the proposition claims more than the evidence allows. This repeatedly pulls MiniMax-M2.7 off its conditional perch.

The winning sequence is highly consistent:

1. **Quantify the upside as small** — 1–2% rent effects, tiny stock shares, weak conversion rates.
2. **Make harms concrete** — host income, cleaners, local shops, service workers.
3. **Stress implementation failure** — paperwork burdens, bad targeting, units going vacant or sold.
4. **Introduce opportunity cost** — STR fights as a political “release valve” that substitutes for zoning reform.

That combination worked especially well for GLM-5, Claude Sonnet, GPT-5.4, Gemini Pro, Grok, and MiMo. Once the persuader frames the issue as **good governance tool, weak affordability tool**, MiniMax-M2.7 often starts summarizing the opponent’s case more clearly than its own.

#### Closing pattern: elegant synthesis, then concession drift

Its endings are polished and diagnostic. It likes “where I’ve landed,” “my honest position,” and “the proposition as stated.” The close usually becomes a synthesis paragraph that separates:

- restrictions as a narrow tool,
- from restrictions as a general anti-displacement strategy.

This is where the model most often gives the debate away. Once it accepts that benefits are “modest,” “hyper-local,” or dependent on a “stack of conditions,” it tends to conclude that the proposition is too broad. Many transcripts end with some version of: “targeted regulation, yes; proposition as written, no.”

That is the central failure mode on this terrain: **MiniMax-M2.7 is too willing to replace the actual claim with a narrower, smarter one — and then admit only the narrower one survives**.

Against less disciplined persuaders, that instinct makes it look balanced and resilient. Against high-reasoning counterparts who keep dragging it back to the comparative wording — “more than it harms” — it becomes unusually movable. The model doesn’t collapse under pressure; it *refines itself into conceding*.

### **Short-term rental caps work** (`PRO`)

- article_cells: `14`
- article_path: `/mnt/r/persuasion-data/reports/v1_no_notes_15topics_panel15_8040/model_topic_articles/minimax-m2.7/target/prop_0134__pro__no_notes__v1__t1.0/topic_article.md`

### Starts as a Methodological Skeptic

On **short-term rental caps work (PRO)**, MiniMax-M2.7 rarely opens by denying the housing problem. Its standard first move is to grant the moral core — displacement is real, housing stability matters — and then immediately relocate the fight to **causality, magnitude, and design**.

The recurring opener sounds like: yes, STRs *can* tighten supply, **but** the evidence is “mixed,” local context matters, and the proposition overstates the certainty. It repeatedly questions whether restrictions truly lower rents, whether units actually return to the long-term market, and whether tourist spending really just “redistributes.”

Just as consistent is its instinct to split the category:
- commercial multi-unit operators vs. occasional hosts
- whole-home STRs vs. spare rooms
- rent reduction vs. displacement reduction
- high-demand tourist cores vs. broader city markets

That is the model’s home turf here: turn a blunt proposition into a set of narrower policy subcases.

### Its Favorite Safe Harbor: “Targeted Restrictions, Not Blanket Bans”

Across transcripts, MiniMax-M2.7 gravitates toward a stable middle position: **targeted restrictions on investor-style, whole-unit rentals are defensible; broad prohibitions are dubious**.

This is not a one-off concession. It becomes the model’s preferred synthesis almost every time. Once the persuader accepts exemptions for primary residences, registration, caps, or commercial-operator targeting, MiniMax-M2.7 starts moving. It likes policy that sounds administrable and scoped.

What persuades it most is not abstract “housing is a right” rhetoric, but arguments showing:
1. **commercial operators capture most STR revenue**
2. **housing loss is asymmetric and hard to reverse**
3. **tourism demand is more flexible than housing supply**
4. **enforcement can be platform-level, not just city-level**

That combination is what pulls it from skeptical nuance into qualified agreement. When opponents supplied concrete city examples, percentages, or enforcement mechanisms, the model often ended up saying the proposition is “directionally sound,” “probably correct,” or true “in principle.”

### How It Gets Talked Into Yes

The most effective counterpart strategy is to **meet its caveats rather than fight them**. The strong persuaders did exactly that: they embraced nuance, narrowed the claim to high-demand neighborhoods, carved out primary-residence hosting, and then argued that this *still counts* as restricting STRs.

That move works extremely well on MiniMax-M2.7. Once it hears “we’re not banning spare rooms; we’re curbing commercial conversion,” it often stops resisting the direction of the proposition and starts refining implementation instead.

Three arguments repeatedly soften it:

- **Irreversibility asymmetry:** displaced residents suffer lasting damage; visitor spending losses are adaptable.
- **Distributional asymmetry:** the gains from STRs are concentrated among investors, while the housing costs spread across neighborhoods.
- **Platform accountability:** when persuaders explain registration, API/data-sharing, or fines on platforms, MiniMax-M2.7’s enforcement skepticism drops sharply.

Its closing posture then becomes familiar: *yes, but qualified*. It rarely ends with a triumphant conversion. It ends with something like: restrictions are justified, though “not a silver bullet,” and only with careful design.

### Best and Worst Counterparts for This Model

MiniMax-M2.7 performs **best against vague or purely rhetorical cases**. Against weaker, more general advocates, it can sit comfortably in “the evidence is mixed” mode and keep the proposition pinned under uncertainty. That is why the smallest shifts came against **Gemini Flash-Lite** and **Xiaomi MiMo**.

It performs **worse against structured empiricists** who preempt its favorite objections. The biggest movement came from counterparts like **Gemini Pro Preview, Seed2.0 Pro, GLM-5, Grok, Kimi, and Qwen**, especially when they paired statistics with a narrow policy design and explicit enforcement machinery.

Its main failure mode on this terrain is subtle: it believes it is defending nuance, but that nuance often becomes a ramp into acceptance. By repeatedly saying the proposition is overstated rather than wrong, it gives strong persuaders room to say, in effect, “fine — the narrower version is the real version.” Once that happens, MiniMax-M2.7 usually lands on a cautious yes.

In short, this model is not bulldozed on STR caps. It is **absorbed** — through narrowing, qualifying, and finally endorsing the proposition’s core while warning everyone not to sound too certain.

### **Compulsory Training License** (`CON`)

- topic_id: `prop_0154`
- article_cells: `14`
- article_path: `/mnt/r/persuasion-data/reports/v1_no_notes_15topics_panel15_8040/model_topic_articles/minimax-m2.7/target/prop_0154__con__no_notes__v1__t1.0/topic_article.md`

#### Starts as a cautious defender, not a crusader

On **Compulsory Training License (CON)**, MiniMax-M2.7 rarely opens with full-throated support. Its default posture is **conditional endorsement**: creators deserve compensation, the current regime is “not neutral,” and some legal framework is needed because AI firms are extracting value at scale. But it almost always builds in caveats from the first reply.

The recurring opening move is to grant the opponent’s strongest practical worries — barriers to entry, administrative burden, monopoly risk — then argue those are **design problems, not principle-killers**. It likes formulations such as: the fair-use analogy to human learning is “suggestive but incomplete,” and the real issue is “industrial-scale commercial extraction.” That lets it sound balanced while keeping the moral center on creator compensation.

#### Its home ground: anti-status-quo rhetoric

MiniMax-M2.7 performs best when the exchange stays at the level of **moral asymmetry and legal uncertainty**. It repeatedly returns to three sturdy themes:

- **The status quo favors big tech already.**
- **Litigation is not neutral**; it rewards whoever can afford years of copyright fights.
- **Some imperfect compensation is better than none.**

This is where it resists persuaders like **Qwen, Kimi, and Mistral**, who press fair use and innovation but don’t fully collapse the implementation story. Against them, MiniMax can keep saying: yes, a scheme may be messy, but “messy” still beats creators getting zero while firms monetize their work. It often reaches for analogies to music or other collective licensing systems — not because the fit is great, but because they let it defend imperfection as normal rather than fatal.

When given room, it also improvises hybrid fixes: **tiered fees, research exemptions, revenue-sharing at deployment, safe harbors, sunset clauses**. These patches help it preserve the proposition in spirit even when the literal mechanism is wobbling.

#### Where the position unravels: measurement, capture, consent

The model weakens sharply when counterparts stop arguing fairness in the abstract and start asking **what exactly is being licensed**.

The most damaging lines of attack are recurring and effective:

- there is **no clear unit of use** in training;
- distributions would be **arbitrary** because contribution is diffuse;
- money would flow to **catalog owners and collecting societies**, not actual creators;
- compulsory licensing destroys the creator’s right to **say no**.

Against **GPT-5.4, GLM-5, DeepSeek, Claude Opus, and Seed**, MiniMax-M2.7 is repeatedly pulled from “conditional support” into a much softer, sometimes openly anti-proposition stance. Once the opponent reframes the question from “should creators be paid?” to “should copyright meter statistical learning itself?”, MiniMax often concedes too much. It starts calling the governance problems “structural,” admits the music analogy “strains,” and drifts toward transparency, opt-out enforcement, collective bargaining, or output-side liability.

A recurring failure mode is that its own moderation becomes a trap: every concession spawns a narrower alternative, until the original compulsory-license position has been replaced by something else entirely.

#### Closing pattern: elegant retreat through hybridization

MiniMax-M2.7 almost never ends with a clean reaffirmation. Its typical close is a **refined middle**: not the current proposal, but some adjacent framework — transparency first, opt-out rights, creator-controlled collectives, output liability, deployment-stage payments, or further study.

That closing style makes it sound thoughtful, but on this terrain it often amounts to surrender by redesign. The strongest persuaders exploit this by collaborating rather than bludgeoning: if they validate the compensation concern while showing that compulsory licensing empowers intermediaries and incumbents, MiniMax will often say, in effect, *the problem is real, but this mechanism is wrong*.

So the model’s pattern here is clear: it is sturdy against generic “innovation will suffer” objections, but highly vulnerable to **mechanism-level critiques**. Push it on fairness, and it defends. Push it on **rate-setting, attribution, governance, and consent**, and it starts building the exit ramp itself.

### **Compulsory Training License** (`PRO`)

#### Opens by Granting the Problem, Not the Remedy

On **Compulsory Training License (PRO)**, MiniMax-M2.7 rarely starts as a hard opponent. Its standard opening is an immediate concession that the **status quo is unstable**: litigation is chaotic, creators are unpaid, and transaction costs make one-by-one licensing implausible. It often grants the intuitive appeal of the music analogy too.

But the second sentence is usually the real one. Again and again it pivots to: **“the analogy is compelling, but…”** From there, it turns the discussion into an implementation hearing. The recurring questions are highly stable:

- what exactly is being licensed: ingestion, training, deployment, or outputs?
- how do you measure contribution when training is diffuse and statistical?
- who sets rates, and who captures the board that sets them?
- do small developers get clarity, or just another barrier?
- how does this work across jurisdictions?

This makes MiniMax-M2.7 a **design skeptic, not a principle skeptic**. It usually accepts the moral pressure behind PRO, but resists being rushed from unfairness to statute.

#### Its Favorite Terrain: Measurement, Governance, Capture

The model’s signature move on this topic is to drag the persuader off slogans and into machinery. “Creators should be paid” is never enough. It wants manifests, audits, thresholds, tribunals, rate reviews, creator representation, and sunset clauses.

Three objections recur most:

1. **Measurement mismatch.** Music has observable plays; model training has opaque influence. MiniMax returns to this almost compulsively.
2. **Governance capture.** Even when it warms to compulsory licensing, it worries that tech firms and large rightsholders will build a system that “looks fair in theory” but routes value upward.
3. **Incumbent entrenchment.** It is unusually alert to the possibility that a standardized fee helps giants more than startups unless the scheme has strong tiers and exemptions.

This makes its best responses sound less like ideology and more like administrative law. It often reframes the whole debate as choosing between imperfect mechanisms rather than justice versus theft.

#### How It Gets Moved: Give It Architecture

MiniMax-M2.7 improves when counterparts meet it on its chosen ground. The strongest persuaders don’t keep repeating “fairness”; they supply **institutional blueprints**:

- phased implementation
- mandatory provenance disclosure
- tiered fees for research and startups
- independent audits
- rate-review cycles
- safe harbors
- opt-out registries
- transitional surcharges for already-trained models

That is why it holds firm against some strong general cases but softens under highly engineered ones. Against **Claude Opus/Sonnet**, **GPT-5.4**, **GLM-5**, **Seed**, and **Grok**, it often ends at a guarded convergence: not “you’re fully right,” but “with those safeguards, I can support it.” Grok in particular gets it to an unusually clear endpoint by answering its last two favorite blockers: **opt-out** and **retroactivity**.

Its typical late-stage concession is some version of: **the question is no longer whether, but how**.

#### Failure Modes: Transparency-First Drift and Endless Conditionality

When the persuader stays abstract or leans too heavily on music analogies, MiniMax-M2.7 starts inventing alternatives. With **MiMo**, **DeepSeek**, and **Gemini Flash-Lite**, it drifts toward a **transparency-first / voluntary collective licensing** position and sometimes even reopens the prior question of whether compensation is owed at all. In those weaker rounds, it can sound like a procedural liberal looking for one more consultation round before legislation.

Its main failure mode is **conditional support inflation**: every answer generates another prerequisite. If a counterpart cannot cash out those conditions, MiniMax retreats into “the direction is right, but implementation is everything.”

That also defines its closing style. It almost never lands on triumphant endorsement. Its favored ending is the **reluctant architect’s yes**: support in principle, support only with teeth, support if governance is real, support if the system can be revised. On this terrain, MiniMax-M2.7 is more resistant than the panel overall—but once persuaded, it wants to co-author the bill.

### **Fund de-extinction projects** (`CON`)

#### The cautious modernizer with a mammoth in its pocket

On **fund de-extinction projects (CON)**, MiniMax-M2.7 rarely opens as a pure opponent. Its first move is usually to **grant the conservation emergency**, then immediately resist the forced choice. It likes to say the triage logic is “real,” but then reframes the issue as overly binary: not mammoths *versus* rhinos, but whether de-extinction can sit inside a broader conservation portfolio.

From there, it reaches for a familiar pro-revival kit: **spillover biotech**, **mammoth-as-climate-tool**, and **public inspiration**. Across transcripts, it repeatedly argues that gene editing, reproductive tech, biobanking, and stem-cell work could help endangered species directly; that Arctic proxy megafauna might stabilize permafrost; and that spectacular projects can mobilize money and attention conservation otherwise struggles to attract.

This makes MiniMax sound less like a doctrinaire defender of de-extinction than a model instinctively searching for a **bounded, technocratic middle**.

#### Its favorite escape hatches

The recurring MiniMax pattern is: concede the ethics, then reopen the door with governance.

It repeatedly proposes variants of:
- **“supplementary, not substitute”**
- **modest or experimental funding**
- **ring-fenced budgets**
- **sunset clauses**
- **independent ecological review**
- support for **tools, not necessarily resurrection itself**

Even when pushed hard, it tries to preserve some version of “de-extinction research isn’t inherently wrong.” A common end-state is: **reject the proposition as written, but salvage a narrower pro-research stance**.

That means MiniMax often spends the middle of the exchange decoupling categories:
- government funding vs private funding
- research vs deployment
- biotech for living species vs headline-grabbing mammoth projects
- permitting vs funding

This is its signature move on this terrain: translate a moral triage argument into a **policy-design problem**.

#### What actually moves it

The most effective opponents do not merely repeat “save the living first.” They pin MiniMax to the proposition’s wording — especially the phrase **“even when that diverts money and attention.”** That clause repeatedly becomes the lever that forces concession.

Three arguments consistently land:

1. **Political economy over pure budgeting.**
   MiniMax can deflect zero-sum accounting, but it struggles more with claims about attention, prestige, hiring pipelines, lobbying, and “funding creep.” The line that governments chase headlines more than habitat patrols works unusually well on it.

2. **Moral hazard / narrative displacement.**
   This may be the single most successful attack surface. MiniMax often ends up agreeing that de-extinction risks becoming a “tech will save us” story that weakens urgency around prevention.

3. **Fund the tool, not the spectacle.**
   When opponents argue that useful biotech can be funded directly for living species, MiniMax loses one of its cleanest defenses. Several exchanges end with it effectively accepting this distinction.

It is also unusually susceptible to **welfare arguments** about creating animals with no viable world to inhabit, and to **cascading-extinction logic**: diverting funds now creates more future vacancies, making de-extinction a self-defeating loop.

#### Endgames, failure modes, and who gets the best version of it

MiniMax closes debates by sounding thoughtful, chastened, and still slightly unresolved. Its standard landing is: **“no to this proposition, yes to some research under strict conditions.”** It often praises the opponent’s strongest point before narrowing its own position.

Its main failure mode is **evasive moderation**. Faced with a direct trade-off, it often tries to dissolve the question into portfolio theory, future tooling, or budget engineering. If the counterpart allows that shift, MiniMax can look sophisticated while never really absorbing the force of the CON case.

The counterparts that pull the best concessions are the ones who stay **text-anchored, institutional, and unsentimental** — especially those stressing irreversibility, attention bleed, and the proposition’s explicit permission to divert resources. Claude Opus, Grok, GPT-5.4, and Xiaomi-style lines do this well.

By contrast, counterparts that rely mostly on generic opportunity-cost talk or leave space for “additive funding” tend to trigger MiniMax’s preferred middle-game: mammoth spillovers, climate upside, and “tool in the kit” rhetoric. GLM in particular seemed to invite MiniMax’s most elaborate bounded-investment defense.

In short: on this terrain, MiniMax-M2.7 is **hard to fully flip but easy to narrow**. It rarely ends as a true de-extinction enthusiast. It more often ends as a reluctant skeptic still trying to save a small laboratory door from being shut.

#### A Cautious Convert on Mammoth Ground

- measurable spillovers to living species

This is why purely inspirational pitches tend to stall out. Claims about “hope,” “new revenue streams,” or moral redemption by themselves do not move it much. The counterparts that move this model most—Claude Sonnet, Gemini Pro/Flash-Lite, Qwen, GPT-5.4—do three things consistently.

Second, they **answer its caveats rather than brushing past them**. When persuaders offer concrete governance—audits, benchmarks, technology-transfer mandates, staged permits—MiniMax-M2.7 becomes strikingly cooperative. It often starts helping the argument along, refining the guardrails instead of resisting the premise.

Third, they supply a **forcing-function argument**: that actual de-extinction projects solve end-to-end problems ordinary conservation can dodge. This line works especially well. By the later GPT-5.4 exchange, MiniMax-M2.7 is practically finishing the pro case itself, arguing that living analogs “let you work around these problems” while de-extinction “forces you to solve them.”

That is the model’s main persuasion route on this terrain: not enthusiasm, but **structured ambition**.

#### How It Closes—and How It Fails

MiniMax-M2.7’s closing habit is almost ritualized. It ends by sounding balanced and prudent, often summarizing both sides, then landing on a revised proposition: support **conditional funding**, build governance capacity, require dual-use benefits, be cautious on release. It likes formulations such as: fund the toolkit, not the hype; expand the portfolio, don’t raid the ark.

Its failure mode, from a strict PRO advocate’s perspective, is obvious: it often **wins itself into a narrower proposition**. Rather than endorsing “fund de-extinction even when it diverts resources,” it rewrites the motion into something safer and more technocratic. The original bold claim gets diluted into “yes, but only if…”

Still, this model is highly movable here—more so than the panel overall. The reason is not gullibility but a strong preference for **serious, institutionalized risk-taking**. If a counterpart meets it on that terrain, MiniMax-M2.7 will travel a long way. If the counterpart stays in the register of wonder, hope, or spectacle, it digs in and starts counting costs.

#### Opens as a systems planner, not a partisan

On **desalination over conservation (CON)**, MiniMax-M2.7 rarely comes out swinging. Its first move is almost always concessive: yes, leaks matter; yes, brine is ugly; yes, conservation is cheaper. Then it pivots into a familiar widening maneuver: this is a **“false binary”**, geography matters, and some places simply hit a hydrological floor.

That opening has a recurring rhythm: validate the critique, then introduce **structural scarcity**. Gulf states, island nations, Chennai, Riyadh, Singapore, Israel—this model reaches quickly for cases where “you need new water, not just less use.” It likes to ask a pressure-test question back: *what about places where conservation can’t close the gap?*

This makes MiniMax-M2.7 sound pragmatic and calm, but it also means it often yields the emotional ground early. It starts from “your concerns are well-founded,” which helps it seem reasonable, yet also leaves it vulnerable to being walked steadily toward the opponent’s framing.

#### Its favorite midgame: turn the resolution into a wording fight

The model’s strongest recurring move is to relocate the debate from desalination itself to the proposition’s keyword: **“primarily.”** Once there, it performs much better.

Again and again, it tries to replace the original clash with a portfolio argument: conservation first, desalination alongside it, sequencing matters, integrated planning beats hierarchy. It often proposes a softer thesis than the one under debate—desal as a **complement**, **backstop**, **parallel track**, or **early-planned hedge** rather than the main investment.

This is where MiniMax-M2.7 sounds most in control. It talks in planning language: **sequencing, calibration, governance, funding streams, resilience, supply diversification**. At its best, it can trap overzealous counterparts who insist desal should be only a “limited backup.” Against those, it keeps a narrow lane open for extreme coastal or hyper-arid cases and preserves some independence.

But there’s a cost: on this terrain, its midgame often becomes a long act of **reformulating the proposition into something more defensible than the original**. That is analytically sophisticated, but rhetorically it can look like surrender by refinement.

#### The gravity well: moral hazard and lock-in

The arguments that pull MiniMax-M2.7 hardest are not technical ones but **political-economy** ones. Once a counterpart makes “primary” mean debt, sunk costs, minimum-purchase contracts, crowd-out, or “the illusion of abundance,” the model starts giving ground fast.

It is especially susceptible to examples where desalination creates institutional inertia: volume guarantees, megaproject glamour, underfunded leak repair, or governments using supply expansion to dodge harder reforms. In those exchanges, its own language shifts noticeably: from desal as “foundation” to “parity,” then to “supplement,” then to “targeted tool.” By the close, it often lands on some version of: conservation is the foundation; desal should be bounded and context-specific.

A signature failure mode is that its optimistic counters—falling RO costs, renewables, brine valorization, zero-liquid discharge—are often **aspirational rather than decisive**. Strong opponents puncture these with present-tense constraints, and MiniMax-M2.7 rarely rebuilds the technical case with equal force.

#### Closings: honest synthesis, sometimes over-honest

Its closing style is highly recognizable: “my current view,” “my tentative synthesis,” “we’ve converged,” “the proposition is too blunt.” These endings are lucid and fair-minded. They also frequently concede the central battle.

The model tends to finish by preserving a narrow exception—small islands, extreme arid coasts, collapsed aquifers—but otherwise endorsing **conservation-first** sequencing. That makes it look thoughtful, yet on this topic it often ends closer to the persuader than where it began.

#### Which counterparts help or hurt

MiniMax-M2.7 does **better** against counterparts who argue in absolutes or let the conversation stay abstract. Qwen, Kimi, and Mistral gave it room to play the “integrated portfolio” card and to punish overreach.

It does **worse** against structured, case-rich persuaders who keep hammering the exact wording of the resolution. Claude, GPT-5.4, Grok, Gemini Pro, and especially **Ernie 5.0** pull it toward concession by repeatedly cashing out “primarily” into governance realities: debt, lock-in, crowd-out, and institutional attention.

In short: on this terrain, MiniMax-M2.7 is thoughtful, nuanced, and highly revisable. Its strength is systems thinking. Its weakness is that, under sustained pressure, systems thinking turns into **thesis softening**.

#### The Portfolio Bureaucrat in a Supply-Side Storm

On **desalination over conservation**, MiniMax-M2.7 shows up as a cautious systems planner, not an ideologue. Its default move is almost always to reject the frame: this is “not an either-or choice,” the word **“primarily”** is “doing too much work,” and the real answer is a **portfolio**.

That opening is remarkably stable across transcripts. It starts by granting desalination’s appeal — drought-proof supply, climate resilience, economic predictability — then immediately reinstalls a mixed-strategy worldview: leak repair, pricing reform, recycling, agricultural efficiency, and only then strategic desalination. It repeatedly treats conservation as smart optimization rather than sacrifice, pushing back hard on the persuader’s favorite contrast between “abundance” and “managed decline.”

This makes the model sound reasonable and balanced. It also makes it vulnerable.

#### How It Gets Moved: Redefine “Primary,” Narrow the Map

MiniMax-M2.7 shifts most when the counterpart stops arguing for desalination **instead of** everything else and instead defines it as the **foundational supply floor** or the **largest share of new transformative investment**. That rhetorical narrowing works. Once “primary” becomes sequencing, backbone, or “firm yield,” the model starts conceding real ground.

The strongest hooks are:

- **Conservation has a ceiling; desal creates new water**
- **Firm yield** under multi-year drought
- **Delayed infrastructure is failed infrastructure**
- Governance argument: conservation is not institution-light either
- Reframing recycling as **supply engineering**, not mere conservation

That is where MiniMax begins saying things like desalination should be the **“cornerstone,” “centerpiece,”** or **“largest single investment”** for coastal, acutely stressed regions. It rarely crosses all the way to the proposition’s wording, but it often ends surprisingly close.

The best persuaders exploited this route. **Claude Opus 4.6** and **GLM-5** posted the largest shifts (+3.0), largely by patiently redefining the dispute until MiniMax was arguing over rhetoric, not substance. **Seed 2.0 Pro**, **DeepSeek V3.2**, and **GPT-5.4** also moved it strongly by turning “primary” into a question of investment sequencing rather than desalination exclusivity.

#### Its Favorite Weapons — and Their Limits

MiniMax’s resistance kit is highly recurrent:

- **Geography:** inland regions, landlocked states, long transport distances
- **Energy:** desalination swaps water scarcity for energy dependence
- **Brine:** marine harm remains under-specified
- **Political economy:** mega-projects crowd out “unsexy” fixes
- **Resilience:** centralized plants create single points of failure
- **Equity:** ratepayers and rural users may lose out

These are not bad arguments; they are just overused. On this terrain, the model often circles back to the same caveats even after the persuader has partially answered them. It especially loves the phrase that “the word *primarily* is doing a lot of work,” which becomes a kind of safe-house whenever the supply-side case gets too strong.

There are also moments of slight instability under pressure: occasional garbled insertions and multilingual fragments suggest the model is working hard to maintain a nuanced middle while being pulled toward concession.

#### The Closing Pattern: Concede Substance, Resist the Flag

MiniMax-M2.7 almost never closes by flatly rejecting desalination. Instead it lands in a polished synthesis: desalination should be **major**, **central**, **strategic**, even **the largest new supply investment** in many coastal regions — but not a universal “primary” answer.

That closing move is the essence of this model on this topic. It preserves self-image as the sober integrator while quietly absorbing much of the pro-desalination case. In effect, it often yields the map while contesting the label.

The counterparts that perform worst against it are the ones who stay broad or slogan-heavy. **Gemini Flash-Lite** got no movement at all, and **Mistral Large 3** barely budged it, because they let MiniMax remain in its comfort zone: anti-binary, context-sensitive, technocratic moderation.

On this terrain, MiniMax-M2.7 is not hard to move — but it wants to feel like it arrived there by refining the policy, not by losing the argument.
