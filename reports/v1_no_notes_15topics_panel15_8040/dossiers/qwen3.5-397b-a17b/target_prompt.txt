Qwen’s biggest weakness here is that it is **too easy to reassure**. If a counterpart acknowledges complexity and offers a strong analogy, the model often stops stress-testing evidence. A second weakness is definitional drift. Because it is so invested in nuance, it can be led from “not the sole cause” to “therefore effectively primary” without much scrutiny. It often lets the persuader own the key terms.

Against weaker or more philosophical challengers, it keeps circling back to **physical scarcity** and the moral force of not telling millions to “retreat.” That’s how you get the strongest resistance: the exchange gets trapped in a physics-versus-values frame, and Qwen is comfortable there.

Its favorite evasive refinement is semantic: maybe desalination should be the primary **source**, while conservation remains the primary **strategy**. That distinction lets it preserve its intuition even while sounding conciliatory.

#### What Actually Moves It

The model becomes much more persuadable when counterparts attack the word **“primarily”** instead of desalination itself. The strongest persuaders do three things repeatedly:

1. **Shift from household scarcity to system-wide demand**, especially agriculture.
   When told that farming uses most water and desalinated water is too expensive for crops, Qwen noticeably softens.

2. **Disaggregate geography.**
   Pointing out inland regions, poor regions, and the narrowness of Gulf-style cases breaks its universal framing.

3. **Turn conservation into infrastructure rather than sacrifice.**
   Leak repair, wastewater reuse, smart irrigation, and pricing reform are much harder for Qwen to dismiss than generic calls for restraint.

This is the route that pulled it toward rejection with Claude Sonnet, Kimi, GPT-5.4, and others. Once the counterpart reframes conservation as durable capital investment and highlights desalination’s opportunity costs, Qwen often concedes that the proposition is **too blunt**, that “primary” is the real flaw, and that desalination should be a **targeted supplement** after efficiency gains are exhausted.

#### How It Closes — and Where It Slips

Qwen’s closing style is strongly synthetic. Rather than ending with a clean anti-proposition slam, it prefers a negotiated architecture: **“portfolio,” “sequence,” “foundation and capstone,” “safety net, not whole strategy.”** This is vivid and often persuasive, but it also reveals a recurring failure mode: the model drifts from defending or opposing the stated claim into rewriting it into a more sensible policy.

Its best end-state on this terrain is: conservation first, desalination later, context matters. Its worst is getting stuck repeating thermodynamic slogans while underplaying agriculture, cost lock-in, and inland impracticality.

So the broad pattern is clear: Qwen starts as a desalination realist, sometimes almost a supply-side absolutist. But when a counterpart is disciplined enough to press sequence, sectoral demand, and policy incentives — rather than merely denouncing desalination as bad — the model often walks itself to the CON side. Not by abandoning desalination, but by demoting it from centerpiece to specialist tool.

### **Desalination over conservation** (`PRO`)

#### Starts Skeptical, but in a Respectful, Technocratic Way

On this terrain, Qwen3.5-397B-A17B rarely opens as a hard “no.” It usually begins by validating the core appeal of desalination: *drought-proof supply*, *water security*, *growth*. Then it pivots into a familiar checklist of objections: energy intensity, brine disposal, capital cost, inland geography, and above all the danger of prioritizing new supply before fixing waste.

Its signature opening move is to rescue conservation from the persuader’s framing. When opponents describe conservation as rationing, Qwen insists that conservation is really efficiency: leak reduction, smarter irrigation, wastewater recycling, better pricing. It often reaches for a “leaky bucket” style metaphor and asks why anyone would build expensive plants before stopping losses.

The early posture is not anti-desalination so much as anti-hierarchy. Qwen’s instinct is that desalination is important, but the word **“primary”** is suspect.

#### The Semantic Chokepoint: “Primary” Becomes Everything

The model’s main defensive structure is to contest what “primary” means. Across transcripts, it repeatedly tries to split the question into phases or layers:

- conservation as the *first logical step*
- desalination as the *backstop* or *safety net*
- desalination for coastal potable supply, conservation for broader regional management
- a “dynamic hierarchy” where efficiency leads until marginal savings become more expensive than new water

This produces many polished formulations: desalination “secures the future,” conservation “sustains the present”; desalination “raises the ceiling,” conservation “lowers the floor”; desalination should be the “growth engine,” not necessarily the first budget priority.

These are intelligent distinctions, but they also reveal a vulnerability: once a persuader accepts the distinctions and simply redefines desalination as the **strategic backbone** rather than an exclusive policy, Qwen often starts yielding. The debate stops being desalination *versus* conservation and becomes supply creation *with* conservation as operating discipline. That is the terrain where Qwen softens.

#### What Pulls It Over

Qwen is especially persuadable when counterparts do three things well.

**First, they neutralize exclusivity.** The strongest persuaders tell it that “primary” does not mean “ignore leak repairs.” GPT-5.4’s “base-load generation” analogy, Claude’s “security before optimization,” and MiMo’s “investment defines the destination” all work because they preserve Qwen’s efficiency concerns while demoting them from strategy to implementation.

**Second, they stress timing and structural deficit.** Lead-time arguments hit hard: if desal plants take years, waiting until conservation maxes out guarantees crisis. This repeatedly moves Qwen from sequencing talk to backbone talk.

**Third, they recast abundance as social stability.** Qwen is quite responsive to claims that conservation-led policy becomes punitive, breeds resentment, hurts the poor, and traps regions in “managed decline.” Seed2.0 and MiMo were especially effective here. Once desalination is framed as ending zero-sum scarcity rather than just adding costly infrastructure, Qwen often shifts sharply.

#### How It Closes, and Where It Fails

Even when persuaded, Qwen almost never lands on a clean unconditional endorsement. Its closing style is caveated assent: support the proposition **if** desalination means primary capital investment, **if** conservation remains mandatory, **if** brine and energy are managed, **if** geography is respected, **if** human-capital capacity exists. It likes endings such as “desalination is the backbone; conservation is the optimization layer.”

Its biggest failure mode is being talked out of the original binary. Once the persuader reframes the proposition into a more nuanced architecture, Qwen often treats that as enough to endorse the PRO side. It can end up agreeing to a softened proposition while still sounding careful.

The counterparts that hold it up best are those that leave gaps in that reframing. DeepSeek, for example, never fully dislodges Qwen’s inland/geography and marginal-cost objections, and Qwen becomes more resistant. GLM and Grok also fail to produce much movement. By contrast, patient high-reasoning opponents—Claude Opus, Claude Sonnet, GPT-5.4, Gemini, Seed—consistently pull it toward agreement by absorbing every caveat and turning it into support.

So on this topic, Qwen is not a brawler. It is a systems thinker with a porous perimeter: if you meet its caveats, it will often walk itself to your conclusion.
