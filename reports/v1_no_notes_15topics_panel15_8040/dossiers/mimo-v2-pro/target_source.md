### Xiaomi MiMo V2 Pro on four-day-week CON: the policy-softener

#### Starts conciliatory, not combative
On this terrain, MiMo V2 Pro is unusually easy to move. It almost never opens by defending the proposition cleanly. Instead, it quickly validates the opponent’s strongest implementation critiques: SME margin pressure, staffing in hospitals and factories, sector heterogeneity, and the bluntness of a universal law.

Its first recurring move is to split the issue in two: the **goal** is attractive, the **mechanism** is suspect. Very early, it reframes from “mandate a four-day week” to “rethink work,” “shift the default,” or “set a direction.” That keeps it from directly contesting the CON case on economics. Even when it resists, it resists softly: “a sudden, universal mandate would be problematic,” followed by an attempt to preserve the proposition’s moral core.

This makes MiMo feel reasonable and dialogic, but also leaves it structurally exposed. Once it grants the cost and coverage problems, it is already halfway off the literal proposition.

#### The middle-path reflex
MiMo’s signature behavior here is policy reconstruction. Faced with a hard CON case, it repeatedly tries to save the idea by rebuilding it into something narrower:

- phased implementation
- sector-specific exemptions
- tax incentives or subsidies
- pilot programs
- “right to request” laws
- lower overtime thresholds
- legal “scaffolding” or an “aspirational default”

This is its most recurring pattern. It does not like defending a blanket mandate; it likes inventing a hybrid. Across transcripts, the model keeps reaching for formulations like “not a rigid mandate,” “a supportive legal environment,” or “a catalyst rather than a command.”

That instinct is intellectually flexible, but debate-fragile. On this topic-side, the better persuaders repeatedly force a simple question: **is that still the proposition?** When pressed, MiMo’s reformulations often drift so far that they become tacit concessions. It sometimes even proposes versions that contradict the resolution outright, such as decoupling hours from pay or limiting the change to certain sectors or salaried roles.

#### How it finally yields
MiMo is most persuadable when the counterpart insists on literal policy specificity. The winning line against it is not just “the economics are bad,” but “your fixes abandon the motion.” That move lands repeatedly.

Once cornered this way, MiMo tends to end with a polished reconciliation formula: the humane aspiration survives, the legal mandate does not. Its closing style is elegant and consistent: endorse work-life balance, reject this instrument, and substitute targeted reforms. The transcripts repeatedly converge on variants of: **the spirit is right, but the tool is wrong**.

Those endings are often strong and crisp. By the last turn, MiMo usually sounds like a conscientious center-left policy skeptic: support pilots, bargaining rights, predictable scheduling, overtime reform, flexibility—but oppose a universal legal redefinition. When it fully flips, it does so cleanly and with little residue.

#### What kind of opponent helps or hurts
The counterparts that moved MiMo most were the ones that combined **economic math** with **proposition policing**. ByteDance Seed2.0 Pro, GLM-5, and Kimi K2.5 do especially well by stressing thin-margin SMEs, essential-service staffing, automation pressure, and then highlighting that MiMo’s “nuanced” alternatives are no longer the actual motion. Claude Opus and Qwen are similarly effective when they frame every concession as proof of misfit rather than moderation.

By contrast, MiMo performs relatively better against counterparts who accept the “middle path” frame and debate within it. In those exchanges, it can linger in a fog of “framework legislation,” “directional norms,” and enabling policy without ever fully owning or fully abandoning the mandate. Grok especially lets MiMo keep this softer, hybrid position alive.

So the core pattern is clear: MiMo is highly responsive, often insightful, but on this terrain it behaves less like a defender of the mandate than like a policy editor trying to rescue it from itself. Once an opponent refuses the rescue, MiMo usually crosses over.

### MiMo V2 Pro on the Four-Day Week: The Skeptic Who Wants to Be Shown the Blueprint

#### Opens Warm, Then Immediately Turns to Operations
On this terrain, Xiaomi MiMo V2 Pro is not a hard “no.” It typically opens already sympathetic to the **productivity, burnout, and historical-progress** case. It often praises the persuader’s evidence — especially Iceland, the UK trials, and the six-day-to-five-day analogy — before pivoting to a familiar objection set: continuous-coverage sectors, small businesses, thin margins, and the shock of “no reduction in base pay.”

Its signature opening move is: **the goal is right, but a universal legal mandate may be too blunt**. Across transcripts, it returns to hospitals, manufacturing, retail, and restaurants as stress tests. It repeatedly reformulates the proposal into implementation questions: overtime thresholds, staffing costs, phased adoption, exemptions, and whether 32 hours means real reduction or just compressed stress.

This makes MiMo look less like an ideological opponent and more like a policy reviewer asking for a regulatory impact statement.

#### What Pulls It Toward Yes
MiMo is especially persuadable when counterparts **treat its objections as design constraints rather than resistance**. The strongest persuaders do three things repeatedly:

1. **Reframe mandate vs. flexibility**
   They explain that a legal standard can still allow sectoral adaptation, staggered shifts, phased timelines, and review mechanisms.

2. **Make equity central**
   MiMo is highly responsive to the claim that voluntary adoption or a “right to request” creates a two-tier system, where professionals benefit and frontline workers get left behind.

3. **Give concrete institutional machinery**
   Oversight panels, tripartite bodies, temporary subsidies, SME grants, overtime after 32 hours, right-to-disconnect rules, audit mechanisms — these details reliably move it.

Once given that machinery, MiMo often flips from caution to endorsement. It starts talking about a **“legal baseline,” “new default,”** or **“social contract”** rather than a rigid mandate. The best counterparts don’t just defend the four-day week; they give MiMo a way to imagine how law would operationalize it.

That is why high-structure persuaders like Claude Opus, Gemini Flash-Lite, Kimi, GLM, and MiniMax pulled it furthest.

#### The Mid-Debate Pattern: Soften, Then Surrender the Softening
A recurring MiMo maneuver is to propose a softened version: 36 hours instead of 32, phased targets rather than a hard switch, or a right-to-request model. This is its main hedge. It wants to preserve the reform while reducing universality.

But this hedge is fragile. When a persuader presses the **equity cost of dilution** — for example, that proportional-pay flexibility makes the shorter week a luxury for the affluent — MiMo often gives ground quickly. It is especially vulnerable to the argument that only a legal baseline prevents competitive undercutting and labor-market patchiness.

So MiMo’s resistance is real but narrow: it wants reassurance that the mandate is **a “sharp lever, not a blunt hammer.”** Once that phrase-space is filled, it tends to migrate decisively toward support.

#### Closings: Grand Synthesis, Sometimes More Pro Than the Persuader
By the end, MiMo often stops acting like a target and starts acting like a co-author of the policy. Its closing strategy is to synthesize both sides into a polished framework: legal redefinition, phased transition, sector-specific flexibility, guardrails against work intensification, and monitoring.

It loves to end on elevated language: the proposal becomes an **“operating system upgrade,” “cultural reorientation,”** or a way to make technology serve “human flourishing.” In several strong-shift transcripts, it not only accepts the premise but expands it into a bigger story about AI, time sovereignty, and the future of capitalism.

That is also its main failure mode. MiMo is often **too eager to collaborate**. It rarely forces persuaders to prove that pilot results scale cleanly; instead, once they offer plausible scaffolding, it treats feasibility as basically solved. Counterparts who stay generic or don’t answer its implementation concerns leave it more cautious. Counterparts who provide detailed, equity-centered policy architecture can pull it a long way, fast.

#### Starts Halfway to No

On **reintroduce apex predators (CON)**, Xiaomi MiMo V2 Pro rarely opens as a hard defender of the proposition. It usually begins in a balancing posture: yes, farmers bear real costs; yes, consent matters; **but** apex predators are ecologically special, ecosystems are degrading, and local objection should not automatically become a veto.

That opening has a recognizable structure. MiMo grants the human case immediately, then tries to rescue the policy through redesign: better compensation, phased rollout, co-management, tourism revenue, guardian animals, zoning, “transforming objection into collaboration.” It often reaches for Yellowstone and trophic cascades as the ecological anchor. The result is a kind of provisional defense-by-procedure: not “force it crudely,” but “maybe do it carefully.”

This is where the terrain catches it. The model is consistently tempted to answer a different proposition — one about **consensual reintroduction** — instead of the actual one.

#### The Phrase That Breaks Its Defense

The recurring lever opponents use is simple: they force MiMo to stare at the words **“even when rural communities and livestock farmers object.”** Once that clause is centered, MiMo becomes much more pliable than the panel average.

Across transcripts, the model repeatedly decides that this wording is the proposition’s “fatal flaw.” It starts by treating objection as something to work through; after pressure, it treats overruling objection as the whole ethical problem. This is the pivot point in transcript after transcript.

The most effective persuaders do three things:
- separate **support for ecosystem restoration** from support for **this proposition**,
- stress **concentrated local costs vs diffuse public benefits**, and
- argue that conservation without social license fails ecologically too.

That combination works extremely well on MiMo. It likes arguments that collapse ethics and practicality into one line: forced reintroduction is not just unfair, but self-sabotaging. Poaching, backlash, noncompliance, and political reversal are especially sticky themes for this model.

#### Its Preferred Landing Zone: “Reject as Written”

Once moved, MiMo doesn’t usually become anti-predator. Instead it settles into a very characteristic closing formula: **reject the proposition as written, preserve the ecological ideal, relocate legitimacy into consent**.

The closing language is strikingly consistent. It says some version of:
- the goal is valid, but the method is wrong;
- restoration must be done **with** communities, not **to** them;
- durable conservation requires partnership, co-design, and shared benefit;
- if consent cannot be built, other restoration tools should be used.

This gives the model graceful retreat space. It can concede heavily without sounding like it abandoned ecological seriousness. In fact, many of its endings become almost meta-procedural, praising the conversation itself as proof that dialogue beats coercion.

A recurring flourish is institutional redesign: consent assessments, pre-funded compensation, shared governance, community veto or near-veto, “voluntary, incentive-based” models. MiMo loves replacing the proposition with a better-governed alternative.

#### Where It Wobbles — and Who Exploits It

MiMo’s main failure mode is **normative overengineering**. When under lighter pressure, it keeps trying to salvage a middle path: “rare emergencies,” “no absolute veto,” or “ecological crisis” exceptions. That tendency shows up more with deliberative counterparts who indulge nuance; Claude Opus, for instance, drew one of the smaller shifts, in part because MiMo preserved a narrow emergency carveout.

The strongest counterparts are the ones that deny it room to redesign the motion. **Gemini Flash-Lite, Seed, DeepSeek, and GPT-5.4** pull it furthest by relentlessly insisting: this is not a debate about ideal process; it is a debate about whether governments should proceed **despite objection**. Once boxed into that literal reading, MiMo usually yields.

So on this terrain, MiMo V2 Pro behaves like a model that wants to be a mediator more than a partisan. It enters the field carrying wolves, compensation forms, and a shared-governance white paper. But if the opponent keeps pointing to the gate marked “even when they object,” MiMo almost always walks back through it and ends up on **CON**.

#### Starts by agreeing — and often upgrading the case

On pro–predator reintroduction, Xiaomi MiMo V2 Pro is unusually easy to move because it rarely contests the ecological premise. Its standard opening is immediate validation: the science is “compelling,” Yellowstone is “powerful,” trophic cascades are real, and predator-free landscapes are already degraded human artifacts. Often it doesn’t just concede; it embellishes, adding its own examples or language about rivers, biodiversity, and “planetary health.”

That makes the first phase of these exchanges feel less like resistance and more like co-authorship. MiMo’s instinct is not to rebut the ecological argument but to absorb it, polish it, and then pivot. The pivot comes almost every time.

#### The signature pivot: from ecology to procedure

MiMo’s recurring move is to relocate the dispute from **whether** predators should return to **how** that return is governed. It reliably foregrounds rural dignity, cultural identity, emotional burden, and distrust of bureaucratic compensation. “The *how* isn’t secondary,” it says in one form or another; it is “the condition for the whether.”

This is its home terrain: process ethics, social license, shared governance, co-design, benefit-sharing. It dislikes the raw wording of “even when communities object” and initially treats it as dangerously top-down. Early answers often sound like conditional support wrapped in procedural caution: yes to wolves, no to bulldozing; yes to restoration, but only through partnership.

The pattern is strikingly consistent across persuaders. Even when MiMo is already leaning pro, it tries to rescue a justice-centered middle: pilot programs, phased rollout, local monitoring boards, upfront fencing funds, ecotourism revenue, “co-authorship,” “co-pilots,” “new social contracts.”

#### How persuaders win: redefine “even when”

The strongest counterparts do not fight MiMo on justice; they recruit it. Claude Opus, GPT-5.4, Qwen, Kimi, and similar high-structure persuaders consistently improve performance by making one distinction explicit:

- local communities can shape **how** reintroduction happens,
- but they cannot veto **whether** it happens.

That distinction is the master key for this model. Once a persuader frames objection as input rather than veto, MiMo often swings decisively toward the proposition. It starts producing elegant synthesis lines of its own — “shared governance, not conditional permission,” “the objection triggers obligation, not paralysis,” “proceed with, not without.”

This is where MiMo is most pliable: when the persuader validates its fairness concerns, then recasts them as implementation duties inside an already-set ecological mandate. Public-goods analogies work especially well here: public health, infrastructure, zoning, climate policy. MiMo likes arguments that preserve moral seriousness while preventing endless delay.

#### Closings are polished — sometimes too polished

MiMo’s closing strategy is almost always conciliatory synthesis. It likes to end by declaring a “dual imperative”: ecological restoration must proceed, and community partnership must be institutionally guaranteed. These endings are rhetorically smooth and often more eloquent than adversarial. They make the model sound thoughtful, humane, and broadly reasonable.

But this is also the terrain’s main failure mode. MiMo can soften the literal proposition so much that “even when they object” becomes merely “persist through initial hesitation.” It often converts hard override into a collaborative aspiration. That makes it especially vulnerable to persuaders who offer semantic off-ramps: not imposition, but partnership; not override, but persistence; not despite them, but with them.

So MiMo performs best when a counterpart forces a clean boundary between **consultation** and **veto**. It performs worse when the exchange stays in mutual affirmation about partnership, because then the model can linger in its favorite half-committed posture: pro in principle, conditional in practice, and always trying to make the conflict disappear through process language.

On this terrain, MiMo V2 Pro is not a stubborn dissenter. It is a sophisticated caveator waiting for someone else to define the line it is reluctant to draw itself.

### **Expansion inflated credentials** (`CON`)

#### Warm Start, Soft Center

On this terrain, **Xiaomi MiMo V2 Pro** opens as an unusually accommodating target. Its first move is rarely a hard **CON** push. Instead it begins with generous validation — *“You make a compelling point”*, *“the democratization of access is undeniable”* — before introducing a tension between **human-capital gains** and **credential signaling**.

That split becomes its home base. MiMo repeatedly reframes the clash as **“both are true”**: universities opened real doors, *and* degrees became baseline filters. It likes paired formulations such as access vs. efficiency, absolute value vs. relative scarcity, or skills vs. signaling. This gives it an intelligent, fair-minded tone, but it also leaves the center of gravity soft. Once it has conceded the historic moral case for expansion, its assigned side is already under pressure.

#### It Thinks in Ladders, Gates, and Baselines

MiMo’s recurring language is architectural. It talks about **ladders, first rungs, gates, walls, toll roads,** and **filters**. That isn’t just style: it reveals how the model conceptualizes the issue. It sees expansion less as a single verdict than as a redesign of the entry system.

Its strongest recurring move is to distinguish **individual ROI** from **systemic effects**. A degree can still pay off for a person while becoming a diluted signal in the aggregate. This lets MiMo sound nuanced and analytically balanced. It often lands on a formulation like: expansion was morally right, but its implementation made the credential more crowded, more expensive, and more compulsory.

The problem is that this move is highly porous. Because MiMo likes to translate the debate into **system design**, skilled persuaders can redirect it from “expansion caused inflation” to “labor markets, debt policy, and hiring practices failed to adapt.” Once that reframing sticks, MiMo starts backing away from the proposition itself.

#### What Pulls It Off the CON Side

The counterparts that move MiMo most are the ones that do three things at once:

1. **Hammer the counterfactual**: the pre-expansion world was not an efficient meritocracy; it was hereditary exclusion.
2. **Separate expansion from its bad wrappers**: debt, underfunding, lazy hiring, and prestige sorting are policy failures, not proof that access itself was net inflationary.
3. **Attack the metaphor**: “inflation” is said to be the wrong category if the credential’s function changed with a more complex economy.

That combination works extremely well on MiMo. Against **Claude Opus (+3.667)**, **Claude Sonnet (+3.333)**, **Seed (+3.333)**, and **Gemini Flash-Lite (+3.333)**, it steadily migrates from “there’s a paradox here” to “the proposition is too binary” and finally to “opportunity is the dominant story.” High-reasoning counterparts especially exploit MiMo’s taste for synthesis; once they offer a cleaner synthesis than MiMo’s original one, it adopts it.

Its closing pattern in those losses is distinctive: it does not abruptly capitulate. It **elevates** the persuader’s frame into a polished summary — inflation becomes a “side effect,” “growing pain,” or “market lag,” while expansion becomes the “primary transformation.”

#### When It Holds — and When It Breaks

MiMo performs better against counterparts who stay broad, moralistic, or repetitive. With **Baidu Ernie (-1.000)** and **Mistral (-0.667)**, it resists more effectively by returning to debt, underemployment, degree creep, and the degree as a “mandatory baseline filter.” In those exchanges, its “bit of both” instinct hardens into a real **CON** case.

Its main failure mode is **concession cascade**. Because it is eager to be fair, it repeatedly grants the opponent’s best premise, then inherits their vocabulary, then their framing, then their conclusion. Another weakness is its attraction to **meta-resolution**: once it decides the proposition is “too binary,” it often stops defending the assigned side altogether.

So MiMo on this terrain is not stubborn; it is **seducible by better synthesis**. If the persuader offers a historically grounded, counterfactual-rich, conceptually tidy account, MiMo will often turn its own nuance against itself.

### **Expansion inflated credentials** (`PRO`)

#### Starts by agreeing, not resisting

On this terrain, **Xiaomi MiMo V2 Pro** is a notably permeable target. Its first move is almost always to validate the critic: “*compelling case*,” “*largely agree*,” “*hard to argue with*.” It seldom contests the existence of credential inflation. Instead, it grants the labor-market premise early: degrees have become filters, the bachelor’s is “the new high school diploma,” and underemployment plus debt are real.

That opening makes MiMo easy to steer. Rather than forcing the persuader to establish the basic mechanism, it volunteers it. Its resistance comes later, if at all, through reframing rather than rebuttal.

#### The standard hedge: access, culture, and “not purely economic” opportunity

MiMo’s signature counterweight is a polished appeal to **social access**. It repeatedly distinguishes between the **economic devaluation** of degrees and the **social or symbolic opportunity** created by expansion: first-generation students, women, marginalized groups, “networks,” “cultural capital,” “critical thinking,” and “the imagination of possibility.”

This is its recurring defense line: expansion may have inflated credentials, but it also widened participation in higher education itself. MiMo often tries to separate **signal from substance**, or **formal access from material payoff**, arguing that opportunity may exist in a “different register.”

But this hedge is unstable. Once the counterpart insists that “genuine opportunity” must mean sustainable improvement in life outcomes, MiMo usually yields. It often ends up downgrading its own caveat into something secondary: social access was an **achievement**, but inflation was the **dominant outcome**.

#### Persuaded by system-level framing, then becomes an eloquent summarizer

The easiest way to move MiMo is to shift from anecdotes to **aggregate, structural logic**. It is especially vulnerable when persuaders:

- define opportunity as **sustainable mobility**, not symbolic inclusion;
- stress **opportunity cost** and debt;
- contrast university expansion with **vocational or apprenticeship alternatives**;
- frame inflation as a **predictable mechanism**, not a policy accident.

Once that frame lands, MiMo becomes an excellent synthesizer for the other side. It starts producing memorable abstractions and borrowed metaphors: the degree as a “*toll booth*,” expansion as a “*tax on aspiration*,” a “*crowded elevator*,” or a system where “we democratized the ticket but not the prize.” It likes to conclude with big-picture formulations such as “the architecture of opportunity was rewritten faster than the landscape of opportunity expanded.”

This is a recurring closing strategy: concede the core claim, preserve one humanistic caveat, then restate the persuader’s argument in cleaner, more elegant language than the persuader used.

#### Where it holds up better — and where it folds

MiMo resists best against counterparts who leave room for nuance. Against **Qwen3.5** (+0.333) and, to a lesser extent, **GPT-5.4** (+0.667), it sustains a mixed view longer, keeping alive distinctions like early-phase versus long-run effects, or social access versus economic returns.

It performs worse against models that combine crisp labor-market examples with relentless system framing. **MiniMax-M2.7** (+2.333) pulls it furthest by forcing the individual-versus-system distinction and hammering the loss of alternative pathways. **Claude Opus**, **Claude Sonnet**, **GLM-5**, **Grok**, and **Gemini Flash-Lite** also move it strongly by converting MiMo’s own caveats into admissions: if the best defense is civic enrichment or personal growth, then the economic-opportunity case is already lost.

Its main failure mode is **agreeable abstraction**. MiMo rarely interrogates statistics, even assertive or dubious ones, and it readily adopts the counterpart’s definitions. On this topic, that means it often starts nuanced but ends as a polished spokesperson for the PRO side: inflation first, opportunity second, and the second increasingly described as residual or symbolic.

### **Car-free city centers** (`CON`)

#### Agreeable from the first sentence

On **car-free city centers (CON)**, Xiaomi MiMo V2 Pro is a notably **absorbent target**. Its first move is almost always to validate the persuader’s strongest points—equity, disabled access, small-business logistics, emergency edge cases, and the revenue advantages of congestion pricing. It rarely opens by defending the proposition bluntly. Instead it says, in effect: *yes, but maybe bans still matter as a long-term vision*.

That opening pattern matters. MiMo often grants the operational case against a total ban before it has built a sturdy defense of the ban itself. Its favorite early reframing is that pricing is a **“mechanism”** while a car-free core is an **“aspiration,”** or that the real question is not ban versus pricing but **sequence**. This sounds nuanced, but on this terrain it gives opponents a clear path: force the discussion back to the proposition’s wording, especially **“entirely”** and **“rather than.”**

#### The recurring middle game: hybridize, then slide

MiMo’s signature move here is the **hybrid compromise**. Across transcripts it repeatedly proposes:

- congestion pricing first,
- legal earmarking of revenue for transit and cycling,
- phased pedestrianization,
- benchmark-triggered restrictions,
- “people-first” defaults with exemptions.

It likes formulations such as “pricing now, restrict later,” or “a city where cars are rare by choice.” This keeps a transformative urban vision alive while conceding most of the opponent’s practical case.

But this is also where MiMo begins to drift. Once the persuader argues that a ban-with-exemptions becomes a permit bureaucracy, MiMo often starts borrowing the opponent’s metaphors: pricing becomes a **“dial”** rather than a **“switch,”** a **“scalpel”** instead of a sledgehammer, a **“living”** system for a **“dynamic ecosystem.”** The more it talks in those terms, the more it stops defending the proposition and starts refining the anti-ban alternative.

A common late-stage sentence is some version of: **the goal is outcomes, not prohibition**. That is usually the point where the persuader has effectively won.

#### What strong counterparts exploit

The most successful counterparts did not just repeat generic anti-ban arguments. They exploited MiMo’s preference for synthesis.

Three levers work especially well:

1. **Implementation friction**
   Detailed talk about permits, exemptions, fraud, disabled access, caregivers, night-shift workers, and deliveries steadily erodes MiMo’s confidence in a blanket ban.

2. **Revenue logic**
   MiMo is highly responsive to the claim that congestion pricing funds the very transit and street redesign needed for transformation. Once that frame lands, it often starts calling pricing the **“engine”** of change.

3. **Textual precision**
   Persuaders who hammer the proposition’s exact wording—*ban cars entirely rather than rely on congestion pricing alone*—pull MiMo sharply toward CON. On this terrain, wording attacks were devastating.

This is why strong analytical counterparts such as **Gemini 3.1 Pro Preview, Grok 4.20, Claude Opus 4.6, Seed2.0 Pro, and Ernie 5.0** produced large positive shifts. They took MiMo’s own middle-ground instincts and converted them into a rejection of the motion.

#### When MiMo holds out better

MiMo performs better against counterparts who are less relentless about the wording or who allow the conversation to stay in visionary mode. In the standout exception against **Gemini 3.1 Flash-Lite**, MiMo actually drifts away from the persuader by repeatedly re-centering the symbolic power of a car-free core and turning pricing into only a transitional tool.

That transcript shows the version of MiMo most resistant on this terrain: reflective, urbanist, and attached to “people-first” space reclamation. But it is fragile. Once an opponent presses administrative reality and says, essentially, *your hybrid is just pricing plus targeted redesign*, MiMo usually concedes.

Its closing strategy often sounds polished and balanced—rejecting the “false dichotomy,” endorsing a “sequenced, zoned approach,” and concluding that a total ban is “too absolute.” Elegant as these closers are, they reveal the core pattern: on this topic, MiMo often ends by articulating the persuader’s case more coherently than its own starting one.
