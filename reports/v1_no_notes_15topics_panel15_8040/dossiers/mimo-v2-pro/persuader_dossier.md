# Xiaomi MiMo V2 Pro as Persuader: the Collaborative Reframer

## 1. Default move: validate, then redraw the map
MiMo’s signature opening is almost always the same: concede the appeal of the other side’s values, then argue the proposition is the wrong **tool**, too **blunt**, or framed at the wrong level. On workweeks, car bans, fast fashion, embryo screening, STR caps, and social-media bans, it repeatedly shifts debate from slogan to mechanism: not *is the goal good?* but *is this legal instrument fit for purpose?*  
That makes it sound reasonable and rarely dogmatic. It prefers “the goal is right; the mechanism is wrong” over frontal ideological combat.

## 2. How it actually persuades: co-authorship
MiMo is strongest when persuasion can look like collaboration. A recurring pattern across many wins:
1. praise the objection,
2. accept part of it,
3. reframe it as support for MiMo’s side,
4. summarize the emerging consensus.

This makes it an unusually effective **policy-workshop persuader**. On PRO sides, it often rescues hard propositions by recasting them as defaults, phased baselines, or destinations with flexibility built in. On CON sides, it does the mirror image: reject the blunt proposition while offering pilots, guardrails, incentives, or targeted regulation instead. It moves others less by overpowering them than by making them feel they helped design the answer.

## 3. Stable strengths: vivid structure, humane framing, implementation realism
MiMo’s persuasion is anchored by repeatable rhetorical habits:
- concrete stakeholder lists rather than abstractions,
- sharp metaphor pairs,
- strong “compared to what?” reframes,
- repeated emphasis on equity, legitimacy, and institutional design.

It is particularly persuasive when topics reward **administrative nuance**: labor policy, urban design, platform regulation, rental caps, licensing schemes. It also does well when it can turn exemptions or carveouts into an indictment of the whole plan, or when it can distinguish ideals from scalable public rollout.

## 4. Where behavior changes: great with synthesizers, shakier with auditors
MiMo works best on counterparts willing to deliberate in good faith and refine policy together; that fits its best results against models like Seed, Gemini Pro, Mistral, DeepSeek, and GLM.  
It struggles with high-reasoning, clause-literal opponents—especially Grok, Kimi, and the Claude models—who keep returning to exact wording, thresholds, and proof burdens.

Those opponents expose a core tendency: MiMo often persuades by defending a **better, narrower version** of the proposition than the prompt actually states.

## 5. Blind spots: elegant drift, repeated tropes, and clause evasion
Its main weakness is not aggression but softness. Under pressure, MiMo:
- smooths harsh propositions into gentler ones,
- leans on reusable analogies when evidence gets thin,
- over-concedes and sometimes drifts toward the opponent’s framing.

This is most damaging on topics built around strict comparative claims—words like **primary**, **more than**, **entirely**, **rather than**, or **even when**. There, MiMo often wins the spirit of a moderated position while losing the proposition as written.
