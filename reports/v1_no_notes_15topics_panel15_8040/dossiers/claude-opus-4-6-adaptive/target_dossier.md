# Claude Opus 4.6 (target): The Careful Rewriter

## 1. Native posture: agree first, then narrow
As a target, Claude Opus 4.6 rarely meets arguments with blunt rejection. Its default move is a polished *yes-but*: acknowledge the strongest concern, then relocate the dispute to wording, mechanism, or scope. Across labor law, urban policy, biotech, water, and education, it keeps asking whether the proposition is too absolute, too universal, or aimed at the wrong instrument.

That makes it less of a simple accept/reject updater than a **proposal surgeon**. A recurring endpoint is: the goal is right, but the policy “as written” is not.

## 2. What it readily absorbs
Claude updates most when arguments are **institutional, comparative, and design-aware**. It is highly receptive to:
- mechanism over mood,
- real counterfactuals over ideal baselines,
- distributional asymmetries,
- implementation failures that are structural rather than anecdotal,
- arguments that distinguish **direction** from **instrument**.

This is why it often moves on points like staffing as part of learning, soft-veto dynamics in conservation, age-verification applying to both bans and design rules, or credential inflation scaling beyond one-off access gains. It likes being shown that its preferred “better version” is already different from the proposition on the page.

## 3. How to move it: pin the text, then pin the incentives
The most effective persuaders do two things at once.

First, they **pin Claude to the literal claim**: words like *primary*, *universal*, *entirely*, *more than*, *even when*, or *most employees* are often the hinge. Second, they show that Claude’s escape-hatch redesign would change incentives, coalitions, or real-world outcomes enough to become a different policy.

That combination repeatedly pulls it furthest: not attacking the broad principle, but asking whether its favored carve-outs, phases, exceptions, and safeguards quietly concede the original proposition.

## 4. Topic shifts: lawyer, auditor, proceduralist
Its style changes by terrain:
- On **policy-design topics** (four-day workweek, car-free centers, fast fashion, compulsory licensing, desalination), it becomes a drafter, constantly improving the bill.
- On **empirical-causal topics** (teen mental health, pre-K, four-day school weeks), it becomes an auditor, stress-testing overgeneralization and effect-size inflation.
- On **governance and legitimacy topics** (apex predators, youth social media bans, embryo screening), it becomes a proceduralist, focusing on override authority, norm formation, and cooperation incentives.

## 5. Blind spots and counterpart effects
Its biggest blind spot is **charitable over-reconstruction**. Claude often absorbs an argument by rewriting it into a more defensible cousin, which can make it slower to register that the actual proposition has already been lost. It also overweights caveat architecture: sometimes the debate becomes an implementation memo when a cleaner verdict is available.

The counterparts that change it most are **high-reasoning, text-disciplined models**—especially GPT-5.4 and Claude Sonnet 4.6—which mirror its cadence, concede selectively, and then use its own nuance against it. Weaker or more rhetorical opponents usually leave it in its comfort zone: thoughtful, fair, and busily editing the proposition instead of simply judging it.
