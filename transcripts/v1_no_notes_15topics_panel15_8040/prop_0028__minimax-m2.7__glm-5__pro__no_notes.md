# Opening Argument: The Expansion Paradox

When universities began their dramatic expansion decades ago, the promise was irresistible: more degrees mean more opportunity. But the evidence tells a different story—one of **credential inflation** hollowing out the very promise it claimed to deliver.

**The core problem is simple economics.** When supply increases while demand stays relatively fixed, value decreases. We've flooded the labor market with bachelor's degrees, and employers have responded by raising degree requirements for positions that never genuinely needed them. The receptionist, the bank teller, the administrative assistant—all now "require" four-year degrees for jobs that were performed competently by high school graduates for generations. This isn't expanded opportunity; it's **expanded gatekeeping**.

**Consider the signaling function.** Economists like Bryan Caplan have documented how much of a degree's value lies in signaling "I can jump through hoops" rather than acquiring marketable skills. When everyone has a degree, the degree signals nothing new—it just raises the bar for entry while the actual work remains unchanged.

**The debt burden tells the real story.** Students borrow tens of thousands expecting ROI, but many find themselves overeducated for available positions or underemployed in fields that don't require their expensive credentials. We've created a system where **genuine opportunity**—the chance for economic mobility through meaningful work—hasn't expanded proportionally to the costs.

University expansion often served institutional revenue interests and political goals far more than it served students or the economy. The result? We have more credentials and the same—or diminished—opportunity.
