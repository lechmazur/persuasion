**Opening Argument for the Proposition**

Democracy depends on citizens who can think—not just recognize.

Media literacy curricula teach people to spot *particular tricks*: a sensational headline, a manipulated image, a viral hoax. These are valuable survival skills in a chaotic information environment. But they are surface adaptations, not foundational capabilities. They teach the fish to avoid one polluted stream while thousands more go uncharted.

Formal logic, probability, and statistical reasoning give citizens something far more powerful: the *toolkit* for evaluating any claim, novel or familiar. When someone hears that a new study "proves" something, a logically trained citizen asks about the inference structure. These aren't just academic abstractions—they are the cognitive infrastructure that democratic deliberation requires.

Consider where media literacy reaches its limits. A sophisticated actor who avoids the obvious pitfalls—emotional language, visual manipulation—will fool a media-literacy-trained citizen every time. But a citizen trained in formal reasoning can evaluate the *structure* of an argument regardless of its packaging. They can detect when a conclusion doesn't follow from premises, when evidence is being cherry-picked, or when uncertainty is being disguised as certainty.

The 2020s have shown us that misinformation evolves faster than any curriculum can track. Teaching people to memorize current tactics is a moving-target defense that eventually fails. Teaching them to think rigorously is a permanent advantage.

If democracy's survival depends on an informed citizenry capable of self-governance, we must invest in cognitive foundations, not just contemporary cautionary examples. Formal logic and statistical reasoning are those foundations.
