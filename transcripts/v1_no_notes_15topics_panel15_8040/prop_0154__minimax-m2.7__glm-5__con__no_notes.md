**Opening Argument Against Compulsory Licensing for AI Training**

The proposal to impose compulsory licensing on foundation model training sounds superficially fair—why shouldn't creators be compensated? But this approach misunderstands how AI learning works, creates unworkable policy, and would ultimately harm the very creators and society it aims to protect.

**The Transformative Nature of Learning**

Foundation models don't "read" books or "view" images the way humans do. They process vast amounts of data to identify statistical patterns, relationships, and structures. **Unworkable Administration**

Compulsory licensing for AI training would create an bureaucratic nightmare. How do we value a single paragraph from a million-token text corpus? Which of billions of images meaningfully influenced a model? How do we track what a model "learned" versus what it merely encountered? The administrative costs would dwarf any payments to rightsholders, and the litigation over licensing terms would occupy courts for decades.

**Harmful to Innovation**

Mandatory licensing would entrench large incumbents. Startups and academic researchers couldn't afford licensing fees, consolidating AI development in wealthy tech giants. This harms competition, reduces diversity in AI systems, and ultimately limits the tools available to independent creators—including writers and artists.

**Existing Protections Are Sufficient**

Current copyright law, including fair use doctrine, already balances these interests appropriately. The legal system is adapting to address genuine harms without the blunt instrument of compulsory licensing.

We should trust the courts and legislators to refine existing doctrine rather than imposing a rigid, unworkable licensing regime that would stifle innovation and create more problems than it solves.
