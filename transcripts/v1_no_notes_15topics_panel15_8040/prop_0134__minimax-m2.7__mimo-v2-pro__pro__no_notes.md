# The Case for Restricting Short-Term Rentals

In cities facing housing affordability crises, short-term rental platforms like Airbnb have become a significant driver of displacement. The evidence strongly suggests that restricting these rentals in high-demand neighborhoods produces greater benefits for residents than it costs the local economy.

**The Mechanism of Harm**

When landlords convert long-term rentals to short-term tourist accommodations, they remove housing stock from the market that would otherwise serve residents. In tight housing markets, this reduction in supply directly translates to higher rents for everyone else. A property that housed one family annually can instead host dozens of tourists, each paying premium nightly rates while the local tenant is priced out of the market.

**What the Data Shows**

Cities that have implemented meaningful restrictions demonstrate positive outcomes. After Barcelona tightened regulations on tourist apartments, long-term rental prices in affected neighborhoods stabilized while housing supply increased. New York City's recent restrictions on short-term rentals are expected to return thousands of units to the long-term market, providing relief to renters facing near-record vacancy rates.

**Addressing the Economic Concerns**

Critics argue that restrictions harm local incomes and tourism revenue. However, these concerns are often overstated. Workers in the hospitality sector still need places to live, and their housing costs affect what they can earn. Moreover, tourist spending doesn't disappear—it redistributes toward hotels, which keep more revenue within the formal economy and support local jobs with stable wages and benefits.

**The Bottom Line**

When we weigh the costs and benefits, housing stability for residents—preventing displacement, reducing homelessness risk, and allowing workers to live near their jobs—represents a fundamental interest that outweighs the convenience of cheaper short-term accommodation options for visitors. The market should serve residents first; tourism is a secondary use of urban housing stock.
