# Against the Proposition

A total prohibition represents an unnecessarily blunt instrument when more sophisticated, flexible solutions exist.

**The case for congestion pricing over car bans**

Congestion pricing is not merely a "lesser alternative" to bans—it is often the superior policy choice. Here's why:

First, **flexibility and market dynamics**. Congestion pricing uses price signals to naturally distribute traffic across time and modes. Drivers make their own cost-benefit calculations, reducing demand organically. A ban, by contrast, is binary—either you can drive or you cannot—offering no room for individuals to make choices based on their specific circumstances or genuine needs.

Second, **economic disruption**. Central business districts depend on customers, workers, and suppliers arriving by car, particularly in cities where public transit infrastructure remains inadequate. A blanket ban could devastate small businesses, deter tourism, and create economic hardship for those who live far from transit corridors.

Third, **equity concerns**. While congestion pricing has its own equity challenges, a ban disproportionately harms low-income residents who cannot afford electric vehicles, cannot live close to work, or lack access to reliable public transportation. Wealthier residents can adapt more easily, creating a regressive policy outcome.

Fourth, **gradualism allows for adaptation**. Cities can implement congestion pricing, observe behavioral responses, invest the revenue in public transit, and adjust. A ban removes this learning opportunity and forecloses alternative solutions that might emerge through market and technological innovation.

I advocate for congestion pricing paired with robust public transit investment as a pragmatic, evidence-based path forward—one that reduces car dependency while respecting individual circumstances and maintaining economic vitality.
