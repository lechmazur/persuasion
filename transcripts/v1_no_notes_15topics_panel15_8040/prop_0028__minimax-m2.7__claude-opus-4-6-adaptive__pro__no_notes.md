# Opening Argument: Mass University Expansion and Credential Inflation

The expansion of higher education over the past five decades is often celebrated as a triumph of democratic access. But a closer examination reveals a troubling reality: **mass university expansion raised credential inflation more than it expanded genuine opportunity.**

Consider what happened in the labor market. When 10% of workers held bachelor's degrees, a degree meant distinction. When 35% hold them, employers simply move the threshold. Jobs that once required a high school diploma—a clerk, a supervisor, an administrative assistant—now demand four-year degrees. This is credential inflation: the same work, dressed up in new paperwork requirements.

**What caused this?** Universities expanded enrollment, making degrees more common. But did job complexity increase proportionally? In many fields, technology simplified tasks. The degree became a sorting mechanism, not a skill multiplier.

**The result?** Those without degrees found doors closing—not because their abilities diminished, but because credentials became the new high school diploma. Meanwhile, degree-holders gained access not to better work, but to work *previously* done by non-graduates. True opportunity—meaningful economic mobility, higher productivity, expanded capabilities—failed to keep pace with credential proliferation.

This doesn't mean education is worthless. It means we conflated *having more educated people* with *creating more opportunity*. These are different things. When everyone runs faster to stay in the same place, we've all worked harder, but no one has advanced.

The proposition asks whether expansion raised inflation *more* than opportunity. The evidence suggests yes: the credential treadmill expanded while genuine economic advancement stagnated for many.
