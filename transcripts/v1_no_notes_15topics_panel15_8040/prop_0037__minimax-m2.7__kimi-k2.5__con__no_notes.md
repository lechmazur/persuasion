# Against the Proposition

**The proportionality problem**

Congestion pricing has proven effective in cities like London, Singapore, and Stockholm. It directly addresses the core issue—too many cars competing for limited space—by making drivers internalize the true cost of their choices. A total ban, by contrast, treats a Ferrari owner and a single mother working a night shift the same way. That's not smart policy; that's blunt force.

**Economic displacement**

Those least able to adapt suffer most from absolute bans. Essential workers who cannot access reliable public transit, small business owners who depend on delivery vehicles, service providers serving residents across income levels—all face disproportionate hardship. Congestion pricing, however, allows people to make rational choices based on their circumstances and willingness to pay.

**Flexibility matters**

Cities are living systems. What works for Stockholm's compact center may not suit Houston's sprawling geography or developing-world megacities with nascent transit infrastructure. Pricing mechanisms can be adjusted—tiered by vehicle type, time of day, emission standards—while an outright ban is a binary switch with no middle ground.

**The political economy of change**

Drastic measures generate backlash that can undo years of progress. Gradual, market-based approaches build public buy-in and demonstrate results that make further reform possible.

Let's solve the problem, not create new ones. Congestion pricing works—let's use it.
