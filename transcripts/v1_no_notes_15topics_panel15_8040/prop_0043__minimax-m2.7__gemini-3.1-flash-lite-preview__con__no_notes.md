# Against the Proposition

The proposition suggests we should prioritize formal logic, probability, and statistical reasoning over media literacy education for the sake of democratic resilience. While this sounds appealing in theory—after all, who wouldn't want citizens armed with rigorous analytical tools?—this argument misunderstands both how democratic degradation actually occurs and how effective education works.

**The battlefield is media, not syllogisms**

Democratic collapse rarely happens because citizens fail to identify formal fallacies. It happens because they consume manipulative content, share misinformation, retreat into echo chambers, and respond to emotional appeals rather than evidence. Media literacy attacks these problems directly. It teaches students to interrogate sources, recognize bias, distinguish news from opinion, and understand how algorithms shape their information environment. These are the specific skills needed to navigate our actual information ecosystem.

**The transfer problem is fatal to this proposal**

Research consistently shows that formal reasoning skills learned in abstract contexts fail to transfer to real-world judgments. Students can ace logic exams and still fall for conspiracy theories, partisan manipulation, and emotional appeals. Media literacy, by contrast, is contextually embedded—students practice evaluating actual advertisements, social media posts, and news stories, building skills that transfer precisely because they're learned in authentic conditions.

**Cognitive development matters**

Formal probability and logic are cognitively demanding. Asking primary school children to master these abstractions risks cognitive overload, disengagement, and the opposite of our intended outcome. Media literacy, appropriately scaffolded, remains accessible from early childhood and directly addresses the media children actually consume.

Media literacy isn't a lesser alternative to rigorous thinking—it's the targeted intervention democratic resilience actually requires.
